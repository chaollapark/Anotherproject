So many terms get thrown around when talking about corporate communications and public affairs.  
The TLDR is:  
Companies need a guiding mission and a best scenario that they strive for. To communicate this to their organization they hire internal and external experts "so everyone can row the ship the same direction".  
There are a myriad of different sub-roles in this category, including lobbyists, journalists, and advertisers.  
Mobility between these roles is very common, also these roles are fantastic because public relations professionals get to be at the forefront of tech and social issues while learning valuable skills.

## Why hire a consultancy?
The first questions that comes when talking about any communications jobs is why not just have our own guy? Why pay exorbitant amounts of money for some consultant who looks like he just graduated?

Actually, for some roles companies do hire in house roles. For example a in house lobbyist, or an internal communication manager and for some media roles often companies do hire. The reason why companies rely on a PR agency for crisis communications, or for strategic communications is that a communications specialist is cheaper and more specialized.

Take for example a strategic document. Even if the strategic communications professional started out as a fresh university graduate, after the third brand management section he writes - surely he will do a better job than the internal comms or advertising talent the company has. The graduate will have had to think through the company mission, what scenarios the company could face and what is the best scenario. Also the document that the young graduate writes will then be (hopefully) reviewed by a communications director and other sector specialists such as marketing experts, former journalists, and social media experts.

Consider this, even if your in house person is a genius, will he write better press releases, do better copywriting, and social media management than a team of idiots that have written these documents countless times, and have at least one person with journalism experience?

## Corporate communications, internal communications and external
Corporate communications can be divided into internal and external roles, each focusing on a different aspect of the company’s messaging. Internal communications focus on aligning the workforce with the company’s goals and ensuring everyone is informed and engaged. External communications, on the other hand, target the public, stakeholders, and media, shaping the brand’s public image and managing relationships. Both roles are crucial, as they help maintain a cohesive narrative both inside and outside the organization, ensuring consistency and trust.

## The fine line between lobbying and communication
Lobbying and corporate communication share common goals—both aim to influence perception and policy. However, lobbying often involves more direct interactions with policymakers, advocating for regulations that benefit the organization’s interests. Corporate communications, meanwhile, focus on crafting and delivering the company’s story to a broader audience, including customers and the media. While these roles are distinct, they often overlap, as both require strategic messaging and a clear understanding of public opinion and policy trends.

## From job alerts to new jobs as a successful candidate
In the beginning, especially if you don't have much work experience, I wouldn't really worry much about which of the roles you take on. Just apply to all job alerts and start somewhere. If you don't like it you'll find another job alert, and if you enjoy it it's a really exciting opportunity to work with interesting people and flexible working hours (often you can completely hybrid working jobs too).

If you want to work in policy, but you only found jobs in digital marketing or media relations or marketing communications it's still good - in corporate communications roles are very fluid.

## Flow of Thought SEO Articles

In almost every case, when entering a PR Professionals job market, all we needed to do was write a **flow of thought SEO article** to rank above everyone else.

Step by step, normally, when one enters a new market, one tries to understand the user intent, the keywords (what words are used to search for the right influencers, as in company), and lastly, what strategy or campaigns will best work to break into the market. In the case of Digital PR and the advocacy market, this wasn't the case.

In every market we entered (except for the DC market), we just needed to do flow of thought exercises. That's where you just write everything you think about a topic – let's say communication – while trying to fill the article with as many keywords "that don't sound strange" as possible. Normally, in the private sector, businesses try their utmost to protect their brand name, so when someone searches for "Global PR," the first thing they see is the branded website. But when companies don't protect their name, an actor like [https://www.lobbyinglondon.com](https://www.lobbyinglondon.com) or [https://www.eujobs.co](https://www.eujobs.co) can just take over the keywords (even without the help of PR agencies).

## An Example of a Professional Site (Politico or Weber Shandwick)

When a company hires good PR agencies to protect their keywords, it is extremely hard to outrank them – let alone outrank them on their own brand name.

The **Politico** website is an amazing example of best practices. If one were to use an SEO tool to find weaknesses in their funnel to attract users, one would be hard-pressed. Most of Politico's keywords are protected by a targeted strategy to make sure words such as "media relations," "government relations," or some politician's name are well covered.

This is an important **reputation management strategy** and brand protection tool as well. Which brand wants to be associated with strange events or an unknown website? A clear example of the real consequences of brand management and crisis communication is the **Twitter blue checkmark crisis**. Twitter gave all users the possibility to buy a Blue Checkmark, and some malicious actors pretended to be Eli Lilly. Eli Lilly, the conglomerate, then proceeded to lose millions of dollars in value because some prankster published a fake claim using the blue tick.

Controlling one's brand is key to **corporate communications**, and not being able to manage what was real from fake stakeholders' communications created a huge crisis where there were actual monetary losses.
(Which actually is great for crisis communication and crisis management professionals because it makes it easier to point to our value.)

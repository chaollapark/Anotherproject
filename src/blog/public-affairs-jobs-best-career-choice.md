### What makes a job a great job?
Purpose, Self Improvement, and meaningfulness. That's what most people search for in an exciting opportunity.

Public affairs jobs offer all three of them. You're working at the very edge of new public policy and technology, even if you are just on the public relations side of the job you see everything happening around you. It's a constant moment of learning, and you're constantly surrounded by amazing people who studied something similar (usually law or political science) but are working on fascinating files. And lastly, your job is meaningful, because no matter how small your role, you are still there while the policy that will change the next 10 years is getting passed.

Public affairs can mean many things, but in each case, be it lobbying or policy analysis you still have great networking opportunities (and also a good salary).

### The Happiest Lobbyist in the World
The government relations part of public affairs has some of the happiest public affairs consultants. Other than the fact everyone (from a public affairs assistant to a public affairs specialist) has access to constant events with free food, interesting (and hot) people, in beautiful locations, lobbying is a fantastic job.

It doesn't matter if you're working in Trade Associations, a lobbying firm, or as an in-house public affairs manager; you'll always be juggling different high-profile tasks.

Even the most dull part of the job, arranging meetings and monitoring public affairs news is interesting. The most boring political debates in the dullest committee is still an arena of ideas where poorly dressed politicians try to get their point across.

### What Can a Junior Public Affairs Officer Look Forward to?
#### The First Year Of Your Career
Public affairs roles can vary significantly - depending on the size of the firm you're working for. The smaller the firm the more varied your work will be. In larger firms things can be monotone or diverse, depending on what kind of clients or what kind of senior account manager you have.

I'd try to do as many different things as possible, ranging from all kinds of monitoring, to writing documents and researching stakeholder relationships. This way you know if you really want to continue this path, or if you want to take on different job vacancies.

The only don't(s) - Don't work for an intelligence firm! It means you'll be writing similar reports your whole career if you stay, and if you leave after a few years you will not have tried out that many pivotal role(s). Just do an internship for intelligence firms - also keep in mind they won't exist in 5 to 10 years.

#### Becoming a Mid-Level Public Affairs Consultant
As a mid-level consultant in the communications industry, you'll be more fixed in a department. You might have already changed firms a few times, and you've had extensive networking opportunities.

You probably already know what you want to do - the only suggestion I'd give you (if you're in a big firm) is to learn how to use the accounting software - this will give you a huge heads-up, and maybe you'll be able to start doing senior public affairs work - also it is a source of power (which will help you tremendously in the future when you're senior).

#### Senior to Director
This is the point where, I personally, would start something of my own. You could venture to create your own government affairs consultancy - or you could build up your career. Whatever it is, at this point of your career you know what you want to do (if you don't you really have to rethink your priorities - otherwise you're doing something wrong!).

The thing is, you work in stakeholder engagement, you write public affairs strategies for huge corporations - you have to have your own strategy otherwise (as you'd say to your clients) "you're playing the game passively, and you're not playing to win."

### In Conclusion: Get Some Experience, Find Financial Stability, Make Your Strategy
Later in your career, as a public affairs lead, most of your job is advising clients on strategic issues or leveraging your knowledge of institutions to help build a strategy.

Given the strategic nature of your work, it would be paradoxical for you not to write some kind of strategic document for yourself. Just do a one-pager with a mission statement, a vision, goals, and objectives.

The same thing you'd do as a government affairs consultant for a client - actually, I'd go further, I'd make a bucket list and use the strategic experience you've accumulated as a public affairs consultant for yourself.

No plan means you're on someone else's plan. A bad plan means you've at least tried to do something.  
Remember that we all have our closing date (death).

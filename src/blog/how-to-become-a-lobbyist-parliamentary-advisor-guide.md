In this series, I'll explore the different paths to become a lobbyist and work in public policy (The best job in the world - **read this article to know why:** [https://www.lobbyinglondon.com/blog/public-affairs-jobs-best-career-choice](https://www.lobbyinglondon.com/blog/public-affairs-jobs-best-career-choice) ).

**The TLDR:** Make a plan, think in terms of scenarios, and it will prepare you for a fantastic career in government affairs.

Whether you're just starting out or considering a transition into lobbying, the world of government affairs offers diverse and exciting paths. A career in lobbying not only leverages your political insights but also puts you at the intersection of impactful policy work and relationship-building. By carefully planning and setting clear goals, you position yourself for a rewarding career that can evolve within government, policy development, or public affairs.

**Road No.1** to work in government affairs is through politics, usually after working as an advisor to a politician.  
The job description is almost the same as a public affairs officer - you do monitoring, you have extremely flexible working hours, you have to understand public affairs, select committee hearings, and the inner workings of power.

I'd say at least 50% of former parliamentary advisors end up being government relations or policy development professionals. Mostly because as a public servant you're faced with countless lobbyists, and once your Boss (the politician) loses his election, it's the easiest job you can get.

### What Is It Like Being a Parliamentary Advisor?
If you don't plan, it's terrible - because most likely, your boss won't be one of the legislative drafting politicians.  
Think about it, each legislative session in any country has hundreds of members of parliament elected; not everyone can be influential in the legislative process, so your boss will likely be one of the people who tow the party line (almost all parliamentarians vote with their party 80%+ of the time).

Though becoming a parliamentary advisor is often seen as a dream job and an entry point into political engagement, if you don't plan, you'll likely become yet another cog in the parliamentary procedure.

You'll write some drafts on policy that no one will read, you'll write something for your boss's parliamentary debate or parliamentary questions, and you'll work on the constituency and your boss's pet project.

### What I Suggest You Do: Give Yourself a Mission
Unless you do something crazy, you'll always find some public affairs manager job after, and if you don't, there is likely an employee assistance program - so you have nothing to lose.

Here is what I think are the scenarios:

1. **Your Boss Is A Highly Influential Politician (Very Unlikely)**  
   Let's take the United Kingdom as an example; this means your boss is a committee chair, whip, or a minister. You're in luck, work hard, and try to get elected someday! If you're this lucky, you're probably not even reading this article, and if you are: GET BACK TO WORK!

2. **Your Boss Is A Normal Politician - He Has A Pet Policy (Likely)**  
   You're in for a tough one! You're not totally free, but you're not totally occupied either. In this case, I'd say take 6 months to take it all in, learn everything you can, but take it easy.  
   After those 6 months, write down your goal - be it public office or something else. Then take advantage of the amazing access you have to pursue your goals!

   If you're not sure what goals to take on, we'll come up with a list of good framework goals in another blog article.

3. **Your Boss Thinks He's Doing Something But Is Lazy (Likely)**  
   You won't know that your boss is lazy until halfway through the legislative session. You'll realize your friends are working on more policy and political matters, or they are drafting more legislation and doing more political work.

   But you are actually lucky! Having a lazy boss and working in policy at the heart of legislative drafting is a boon. You'll have all the time in the world to focus on your goals.

### Conclusion
In conclusion, working in government policy is amazing! Working in Government Affairs is even better!  
By working hard and learning everything you can in legislative drafting, you'll prepare a career in government relations while also keeping the door open for a more political role in the future.

You might work on privacy policy or any other subject; everything is good experience and will prepare you. The most important thing is to plan and think of a goal. If you want to work in lobbying for a few years (till you become a senior public affairs officer) or something else, everything starts with a plan.

The beauty of working in government is you know the "closing date", you know when the music stops, and you know how long you have.

To wrap it all up, the journey to becoming a lobbyist or a government relations expert is one that requires careful planning, strategic goal-setting, and a willingness to seize the opportunities that arise within the corridors of power. Working as a parliamentary advisor or in government policy can give you invaluable insights and connections that are instrumental in the world of lobbying. Also, legislative drafting is fun!

Remember, whether your boss is influential or not, the experience you gain and the networks you build can be the foundation for a successful career in lobbying or public affairs. Each policy drafted, committee hearing attended, and connection made adds to your arsenal. So, use your time wisely, stay focused on your mission, and never stop learning. A well-defined goal and a proactive approach are what set you apart and make the transition into lobbying not just possible, but rewarding.
